European Corporate Tax Rates and Tax Havens¶

Abstract¶

There is a downwards trend in corporate tax rates going on for the past 15 years. This is known as the 'Race to the bottom'. This may be due to the existence of tax havens, or more intense competition between countries. The LuxLeaks and Panama Papers did not cause a change in tax rate setting.

Question ¶

Is the trend of European corporate tax rates affected by tax havens?
Press here to quickly jump to the answer.

Introduction & Motivation¶

Over the past few decades, there has been a lot of controversy in the media concerning so called 'tax havens'. Large multinational firms hire accountants from the 'Big-4' accounting companies to think of clever tricks and complicated organizational structures. By doing this, the firms are able to reduce their worldwide tax bill to an absolute minimum. When these multinationals or the accounting firms are asked for a response, their defense typically is that everything they did was legal. They're not wrong, the measures taken are extremely complicated and perfectly legal. The Luxembourg Leaks investigation by the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists (ICIJ) showed that these multinationals were able to lower their effective tax rate to 1%, or even lower than that in some cases.

It isn't only firms that are capable of doing this. More investigations showed that many celebrities, world leaders and criminals found ways to keep their wealth through tax avoidance. The ICIJ uncovered a massive amount of documents called the Panama Papers that revealed all this. In 2017, the ICIJ discovered even more. The Paradise Papers revealed offshore interests of more than a hundred firms and elite figures such as Queen Elizabeth and members of Donald Trump's administration.

As a MSc Economics student in the 'Public Policy' track I find it interesting to what extent firms will go to reduce their tax bills and what these measures look like. I'm particularly interested in if the marginal tax rate is a tool that tax havens use to attract firms or other special rulings and deduction methods. Moreover, how do other countries respond when determining their corporate tax rates to fight in such a competitive environment. In addition to that, is there a change in corporate tax rates set by these 'tax havens' after the revealed documents?

Tax Havens¶

The European Union has made an official list of what they call 'non-cooperative jurisdictions for tax purposes'. At that time, there were seventeen tax jurisdictions on the list. However, there is criticism as to why some of the EU member states such as Luxembourg, Ireland and the Netherlands are not on this list. These three countries were known for being favorable to reduce your global tax bill. To take a look at these three countries and their tax rates, see the following graph:

This goes to show that there are more factors that determine the tax bill than just the corporate tax rate.

In the first graph we have the tax rates for Panama and a few other tax regions. The graph shows that Panama's corporate tax rate has been well above the average tax rates around the world. It has remained very stable, only seeing one drop from 30% to 25%. Even then, the country doesn't follow the same trend as much as the European Average. The dotted black line represents when the Panama Papers were published. After this happened, there has not been any noticable change in corporate tax rates in both the country or around the world.

The second graph shows the same plot, but then for Luxembourg. Again, the dotted line shows the time all the secret documents were uncovered and in this case that is the Luxleaks. Just like Panama, Luxembourg is far above the corporate tax rates of other regions. Luxembourg hasn't moved towards the global average like Panama did. It has remained fairly stable above it. These two graphs show that the tax havens don't attract firms using the corporate tax rate, if that were the case the opposite would be true: almost no business in these countries. They also show that after all these documents are available to the public, the countries don't seem to change their behavior in terms of the tax rate.

All the 'non-tax havens' also don't change their tax rate setting after these publications. What we do see is the downwards trend of corporate tax rates that I mentioned earlier, this 'Race to the bottom' could be caused by the existence of these tax havens. The downward trend could be to fight back against these tax havens attracting their firms to make their own country more attractive.

Conclusion¶

The past 15 years has shown that there is a downwards trend when it comes to corporate tax rates. Whether this is because of tax havens, tax competition or because of the economic crisis remains the question. That is beyond this short investigation of the two tax scandals and tax trends. I can definitely conclude that the corporate tax rate is not the main reason for being classified as a 'tax haven' and many other factors play a role.

To answer the question, the downwards trend in tax rate might be due to increased competition from tax havens. The tax rates after publication of the documents does not seem to be affected.
